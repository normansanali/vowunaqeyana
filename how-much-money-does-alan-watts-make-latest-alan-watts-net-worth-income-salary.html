Alan Wilson Watts (6 January 1915 – 16 November 1973) was a British-born philosopher, writer, and lecturer who is best known for popularising Eastern philosophy in Europe. He travelled to the United States in 1938 and began Zen study in New York, where he was born in Chislehurst. He earned a master’s degree in theology from Seabury-Western Theological Seminary, where he was pursuing a career. Watts became an Episcopal priest before leaving the priesthood in 1950 to join the faculty of the          American Academy of Asian Studies in California. While volunteering as a programmer at KPFA, a Pacifica Radio station in Berkeley, Watts developed a considerable following in the San Francisco Bay Area. Watts authored more than 25 books and essays on topics related to Eastern and Western religion, including The Way of Zen (1957), one of the first bestselling Buddhist publications. Watts proposed in Psychotherapy East and West (1961) that Buddhism could be considered a sort of psychotherapy rather than a religion.
